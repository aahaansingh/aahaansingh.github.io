Minor League Baseball is a wonderful form of entertainment.
Unlike the majors, it is both highly accessible and dirt cheap.
Teams will put on all sorts of wacky promotions, like mascot races, [world record pushes](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lpiFLhsU34w), and [veggie-themed celebrations](https://www.mlb.com/news/minor-league-promotions-for-april-2026).
There is also something refreshing about the lower standard of baseball on display.
The certainty of a play's outcome, at least at this level, is inverse to the skill of the players involved, which makes games both exciting affairs and exercises in patience.

Analogizing the baseball system to American politics requires a generous degree of interpretation.
Is state politics still the major leagues?
Is big city politics?
These are not questions that I am interested in answering, because no matter how you slice it, university politics is the

of the political leagues.
It is about as facetious, as guileless, and as unimportant as politics can possibly be.
I have already documented the case of my own university's would-be

and now I will turn to a new case that is equally representative: the elections for next year's Undergraduate Student Government slate.

There are four parties running in this most important of races:

-   The CORE Collective: These are the _crème de la crème_ of the [bureaucrats](https://aahaansingh.github.io/posts/post8/#we-really-do-have-a-problem) on College Council, let by College Council Representative Kevin Guo. Kevin was the driving force behind the impeachment proceedings. The party supports and is actively pursuing initiatives like allowing Maroon Dollars to be spent on laundry (a noble goal) and establishing the [Judicial Council](http://localhost:1313/posts/post8/#how-not-to-do-it) (which might be overkill, given that USG already has a hard time staffing committees).
-   The Premium Party: More bureaucrats, but less competent. They are led by Grace, the CC rep who [blamed](https://chicagomaroon.com/50737/news/college-council-recap/college-council-recap-members-contest-rso-funding-guidelines-usg-to-distribute-bike-locks/) new representatives for RSO funding issues. Grace herself sits on the SGFC, the largest funding committee in USG, from which she has been absent for [30% of meetings](https://chicagomaroon.com/51667/viewpoints/op-ed/usg-executive-vp-alex-fuentes-dont-rank-premium-or-new-generation/).
-   The Ida Noyes Party: A mix of USG members and outsiders advocating for increased efficiency and for a plan to save [The Pub](https://uofcpub.com/) under Ida Noyes from closing. There is [no indication](https://chicagomaroon.com/51663/news/meet-the-candidates-in-the-spring-usg-election/) that The Pub is in danger of temporary or permanent closure.
-   New Generation Party: A bunch of guys from the Chicago Thinker, our school's right-wing publication. Their candidate for president, Will, is the Thinker's "Co-Social Chair." [Here](https://thechicagothinker.com/uchicago-where-love-goes-to-die/) [are](https://thechicagothinker.com/the-rights-romantic-rebellion/) [some](https://thechicagothinker.com/deport-illegal-immigrants-protect-those-who-followed-the-rules/) recent gems that the Thinker has {{< sidenote "published." >}}And I am pretty sure that the second of those is AI generated.{{< /sidenote >}} They also have a [vibe-coded](https://foranewgen.com/) website and a polished social media presence to push their platform of making UChicago cool and bringing more jobs to campus.

There was recently a debate between these parties, hosted by the Maroon.
It's not worth recapping in full because there was a lot of fluff, but there were some important points of contention.
The major one was funding transparency.
All parties are in favor of funding transparency and they all devoted a lot of time to talking about how they would make the budget publicly available.
College Council had already passed legislation to require that USG [publish](https://chicagomaroon.com/51519/news/college-council-recap/college-council-recap-usg-amends-election-code-ahead-of-elections-passes-resolution-for-rso-funding-transparency/) the previous year's budget (in addition to centralizing communications), weeks before the
{{< sidenote "debate." >}}This was a pleasant surprise to me, because while it's an easy fix, I did not have any faith in CC. It remains to be seen if they will actually implement this legislation.{{< /sidenote >}}
It gladdens me that we are getting some transparency.

The other major issue was budget allocation.
Most of the parties weren't pushing for any radical changes, but New Generation wants to create a Career Advancement Committee with a [budget](https://chicagomaroon.com/51667/viewpoints/op-ed/usg-executive-vp-alex-fuentes-dont-rank-premium-or-new-generation/) of "a few hundred thousand dollars," as well as a shuttle downtown to be funded with $53,000 from the PCC budget.
Will delivered this pitch with the air of one who finds themselves very convincing.
He would also like to give RSOs a party stipend, which sounds like a worse way of just giving them the
{{< sidenote "money." >}}I understand that the point is to make UChicago "where fun goes to live," but if an organization can't be bothered to host parties, they can't be bothered to host good parties.{{< /sidenote >}}
Will has suggested that RSOs are not fundraising enough and that USG could cut waste from the budget, pointing to the $10,000 that they paid for a table a few years back.
However, the vast majority of the USG budget goes to RSOs and there is absolutely no way that, even if they juice the hell out of non-RSO expenses, they can avoid drastic cuts to RSO budgets.
I would expect them to be somewhere on the order of 10%.

Recall that RSOs are already cash-strapped and that the Career Advancement department exists.
The cost to students of taking something like 10% of an RSO's budget (and keep in mind how important RSOs are to building careers, among their many other functions) is surely much greater than the benefit of giving that money to Career Advancement, which has a sufficient budget.
If it doesn't, we're probably better off lobbying the university anyways, but the I doubt it because the people funding the university have a vested interest in its prestige.
Furthermore, it is unlikely that cuts will be applied evenly, so it is worth asking whom they would be applied to.
I cannot say that I trust the judgement of New Generation in this regard.

Meanwhile, USG is in talks with administration to increase the budget, which currently stands at about two million dollars.
The initial request of $350,000 was rejected out of hand, though Kevin insisted at the debate that this was a highball offer.
Admitting this in public diminishes his leverage, but he probably couldn't think of another way to save face.
The CORE Collective may end up getting nothing done, but this will not be for a lack of effort.

The Ida Noyes Party and the Premium Party have very little distinguishing them, although the former succeeded in raising their stocks at the debate.
Their presidential candidate, Daniel de _Beer_, gave an impassioned speech about the importance of The Pub to the UChicago community.
As a veteran of two Pub Trivia Nights, I was greatly moved.
He promised to create more technical roles in USG.
As a CS major (in this job climate), I was greatly moved.
Despite this rhetoric, though, I have a hard time believing that his party represents any sort of change from before, because both pillars of its campaign are redundant.
The Premium Party certainly doesn't—they looked so timid at the debate that I don't think that they've convinced themselves of their own necessity.

USG is doomed to be uninteresting because it has an uninteresting job.
The only thing that could justifiably generate excitement for it would be an increased mandate or gross mismanagement.
Without more power, it can only try not to muddle up the budget, make slow progress on student issues, and streamline internal processes.
In other words, it can be competent and nothing more.
