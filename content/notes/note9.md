## Music {#music}

[NME on the Internet Archive](https://archive.org/search?query=creator%3A%22NME+-+New+Musical+Express%22&sort=-addeddate): NME sits at the bottom tier of music publications.
It is an absolute slop farm that is even worse than Rolling Stone.
Even Teen Vogue has better music criticism.
In the '70s and '80s, though, they ran some really interesting essays and interviews.
Siouxsie has quite a few, as does John Lydon.
[Here's](https://archive.org/details/nme-19771203/page/6/mode/2up) a fun one on The Sex Pistols' obscenity trial, in which an expert witness discusses the origin of the word "bollocks."
It's sad that you don't see these sorts of features, or that famously irreverent tone, as much in today's music journalism.

[Makeup Is A Lie](https://www.nationalreview.com/2026/03/morrissey-unmasks-the-big-lie/): I thought this song was a joke when I first heard it (and saw the album cover) but apparently there are people who take it seriously.
I know nothing about movies so I can't say much about Armond White's worthiness as a critic in that regard, but clearly he is not worth reading on music.
He would have us think that Morissey's idiotic wails of "makeup is a lie" (which make up something like three-quarters of the song) and the song's backing-track production represent a "principled approach" and  "new way of remaking/remodeling pop music."
This album's hamfistedly conservative lyrics have shades of PiL's latest, which perhaps explains the praise coming from The National Review, but even if you ignore the lyrics, it's terrible music.
These are the people laughing at Taylor Swift fans!


## Tech {#tech}

[Why Craft-Lovers Are Losing Their Craft](https://writings.hongminhee.org/2026/03/craft-alienation-llm/): An interesting obervation regarding how AI is separating people who like having things made and people who like making things.
I don't think that this is a hard division (I know that I fall somewhere in between, and it varies based on what I'm making), but it is a real one.
I also get the sense that the population of coders has been shifting from people who enjoy the craft to people who enjoy the output.

[Inverse Square Root](https://h14s.p5r.org/2012/09/0x5f3759df.html): This code came up in a history class, of all things.
It's essentially using float-integer conversion plus a log approximation function to quickly compute an inverse square-root three times faster than the standard library function.
It's outdated now but it's pretty cool that a random game developer came up with this thing.


## History {#history}

[The Fremen Mirage](https://acoup.blog/2020/01/17/collections-the-fremen-mirage-part-i-war-at-the-dawn-of-civilization/): Bret Devereaux's blog is probably my favorite one out there.
I don't know how he manages to write about interesting subjects in such depth so regularly.
This is my favorite of his, a series of posts on the delusion of the cyclical nature of civilization (hard men create good times, etc.).
He both attacks the premise and traces the motivations behind the theory, which is usually promulgated by people _within_ that society who are trying to effect some sort of change.
In other words, the same reason why that point is made today.

[The Glass Delusion](https://publicdomainreview.org/essay/fear-and-fragility-the-glass-delusion-and-its-history): Apparently, Charles VI of France was doing a dance with some nobles while dressed up in tar and then a bunch of them caught fire and burned to death.
Then Charles went crazy and came to believe that he was made of glass.
This essay documents the glass delusion very well.
I wonder why it disappeared—the theory presented, that glass became less weird, makes sense but feels incomplete.
I find it plausible that a cause of this was a change in connotation, since this would alter the physical metaphor of believing oneself to be made of it.
Beyond presumably seeming an odd material, though, glass would have been associated with
{{< sidenote "fragility" >}}More so than now, when our glass is stronger.{{< /sidenote >}}
and wealth.
Maybe it evoked the qualities of stained glass windows.
It's an interesting question, but not a very satisfying one, since with such anomalous events, chance always plays an outsize role.
